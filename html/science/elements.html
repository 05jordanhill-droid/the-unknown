Heat Absorption: As the atomic structure breaks down, it creates a "vacuum" effect, drawing in heat from the environment. This compensates for the inability of the metal to retain energy internally.

Deconstruction: Over time, the material disintegrates as more atomic bonds are broken, reducing the metal to nothing.

Density & Reactivity:

Denser Metals: Last longer due to stronger atomic frameworks.

More Reactive Metals: Absorb heat more intensely due to greater initial instability in their atomic structure.

Extent of the property: This effect can range anywhere from only cold enough to sting when touching for too long to produce enough cold to supercool Nonmetals.

But if the electricity doesn't exceed the Coulomb barrier then the material reacts the same as Alkali Metals.

This collapsed atomic makeup makes an INCREDIBLY dense and sharply precise material. The electrons being bound to the nucleus prevents the material from heating or cooling down.

Actinoids / Oxygen Eaters

Properties of Actinoids in the ORC Reality:

Kinetic Force Trigger:

Actinoids in the ORC Reality are triggered into a reactive state when a certain level of kinetic force is applied. The greater the atomic number, the less kinetic force is required to initiate the reaction.

Upon triggering, the Actinoid becomes increasingly unstable and begins to consume oxygen particles to stabilize itself. This leads to a struggle for balance, as the structure of the Actinoid breaks down but is temporarily stabilized by the absorption of oxygen.

Oxygen Absorption and Structure Breakdown:

The Actinoids' instability causes them to suck in oxygen as they attempt to maintain their structure. As they absorb oxygen the heat and energy is drained from the Oxygen, they gain temporary structural stability from the energy sucked and the physical Oxygen particle that is now supercooled due to having all of its energy drained suddenly.

However, this stability is short-lived, and the Actinoid will break down rapidly, continuing to consume oxygen until it either runs out or the structure becomes unsustainable and it crumbles.

Impact of Atomic Number on Behavior:

Lower Atomic Number Actinoids (e.g., Thorium, Protactinium): These elements are more stable and consume oxygen slowly. They require larger quantities to be effective in reactions but last much longer before breaking down. Their reactions are more gradual, leading to prolonged energy release and a delayed breakdown.

Higher Atomic Number Actinoids (e.g., Uranium, Plutonium): These elements are more unstable and consume oxygen rapidly, reaching their critical point much faster. They are most effective in smaller quantities and result in shorter-lived, explosive reactions. Their instability leads to violent explosions and rapid breakdowns when they run out of oxygen.

Practical Reactions and Applications:

Lower Atomic Number Actinoids:

Ideal for controlled energy release over extended periods, where a gradual buildup and delayed reaction are desired.

Can be used in systems requiring long-term stability and gradual breakdown, such as energy storage systems or situations where reactions need to be sustained for longer durations.

Higher Atomic Number Actinoids:

Perfect for applications requiring rapid energy release or instantaneous reactions, such as high-powered reactors, weapons, or environments where explosions or fast breakdowns are beneficial.

Their violent explosions and short-lived reactions make them ideal for high-energy applications but require careful handling to prevent premature breakdown.

Specific Reaction to Water (H2O):

The Actinoid is capable of pulling the Oxygen particles from H2O when submerged in water. This will leave pure hydrogen behind and when (it will) the Hydrogen comes into contact with the supercooled Oxygen from the Actinoid the Hydrogen will explode violently.

The Hydrogen explosion will place additional pressure on the Actinoid, though the Oxygen particles will soften the explosion on the Actinoid and apply the pressure in such a way that the Actinoid compresses to pull in Oxygen particles even faster.

Once the Actinoid has exhausted the available oxygen, it falls apart due to its instability and is left vulnerable to violent explosions when the pure hydrogen (from H2O) or any oxygen present interacts with the Actinoid.

By this point much of the water left will have become Hydrogen gas and explode, leaving little to no water left depending on the size of the body of water.

Summary of Actinoid Properties:

Triggering Event: Actinoids react to kinetic force, with the amount of force required inversely related to their atomic number (i.e., higher atomic numbers require less force to trigger a reaction).

Oxygen Consumption: Upon destabilization, Actinoids begin to consume oxygen particles and drain them of their heat and energy to maintain their structure temporarily resulting in the Oxygen particle being supercooled. This absorption leads to a struggle for balance, as their structure inevitably deteriorates.

Variation Based on Atomic Number:

Lower Atomic Number Actinoids consume oxygen slowly and are stable for longer periods, making them suitable for long-term reactions.

Higher Atomic Number Actinoids consume oxygen rapidly and break down faster, making them ideal for quick, explosive reactions but require smaller quantities to avoid premature failure.

When a nonmetal is supercooled in your reality, it undergoes a frostbite-like threshold that freezes its atomic and molecular bonds into a new configuration.

This irreversible "freezing" causes the atoms to lock into place with a less tightly woven structure, distinct from their usual covalent or molecular bonding.

Loosening Bonds:

The supercooling reduces the vibrational energy of the nonmetal's atomic lattice to near-zero. This allows for a partial delocalization of its outer electrons, similar to the electron "sea" in metallic bonding, but less free-flowing.

The loosened bonds don't entirely fall apart (preserving structural integrity) but create pockets of semi-free electrons that enable unique electromagnetic interactions.

Fluxon Magnetization:

In this altered state, the material interacts with Fluxons by aligning its electronic domains to respond to their presence. While not identical to ferromagnetism (which involves the alignment of unpaired electron spins), this process creates a form of pseudo-magnetism where:

The supercooled material becomes a conductor for Fluxons, guiding their motion like a rail.

It exhibits selective attraction to other nonmetals and fluxon-sensitive materials, mimicking magnet-like behavior.

Comparison with Ferromagnetism:

Unlike ferromagnetic materials like iron, the supercooled nonmetals don't depend on traditional exchange interactions (the quantum phenomenon aligning spins in ferromagnets).

Instead, the pseudo-magnetic effect arises from the static alignment of their atomic bonds and the interactions with Fluxons, whose spiral motion and energy influence the electron cloud alignment.

Unique Properties:

This "magnetization" is specific to Fluxons and other nonmetals, meaning these materials can interact with Fluxon flows in ways ferromagnetic materials cannot.

Their magnetic qualities may weaken in environments that disrupt their frozen bond structure or deplete Fluxons.

Stable Range of Masses: No stable isotopes, all isotopes are radioactive.

Melting Point: 500°C (predicted)

Boiling Point: 1300°C (predicted)

Reaction Post-Activation:

Tennessine is a synthetic element that reacts with oxygen to form tennessine oxide (TsO₂). Upon activation with a Fluxon particle, tennessine would melt into a silvery liquid at around 500°C. It would begin reacting with oxygen, forming an oxide layer. The activation would cause tennessine to melt, and oxidation would take place gradually. The reaction with water is minimal, but it could react with acids, releasing hydrogen gas. The oxidation process would proceed as the molten tennessine cools.

Boiling Point: 250°C (predicted)

Oganesson is a synthetic element and the heaviest noble gas known, but it is predicted to behave like a solid at room temperature under certain conditions. Under activation with a Fluxon particle, if it were to remain in a solid state (predicted to melt at around 50°C), it would act similarly to other noble gasses. The molten oganesson might remain inert, with oxidation not progressing significantly.
